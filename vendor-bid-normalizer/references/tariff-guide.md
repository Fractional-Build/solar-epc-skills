# Solar Equipment Tariff Reference

This file provides context on tariff exposure for common Solar EPC equipment
categories. Tariff rates and policies change. This reference reflects the
landscape as of early 2026 — always confirm current rates with a freight
forwarder or trade compliance advisor before making procurement decisions.

---

## PV Modules

### Section 201 Safeguard Tariffs
Originally imposed in 2018 on crystalline silicon PV cells and modules.
- Rate has stepped down over time
- Bifacial module exemption was removed and reinstated at various points —
  confirm current bifacial status before quoting
- Modules assembled in the US from imported cells are subject to different
  treatment than fully imported modules

### AD/CVD (Anti-Dumping / Countervailing Duties)
- Historically applied to modules from China and Taiwan
- Circumvention findings have extended AD/CVD exposure to modules
  manufactured in Cambodia, Malaysia, Thailand, and Vietnam using Chinese
  cells — confirm current scope with trade counsel
- AD/CVD rates vary by manufacturer and change through administrative reviews

### UFLPA (Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act)
- Creates a rebuttable presumption that goods from Xinjiang involve forced labor
- Affects solar modules due to polysilicon supply chains
- Importers may need to demonstrate supply chain due diligence
- Some shipments have been held at port pending UFLPA review
- Recommend requesting supplier UFLPA compliance documentation as part of
  procurement — do not wait until equipment is at port

### Practical Guidance for Procurement
When requesting quotes for modules, ask vendors to confirm:
1. Country of cell manufacturing
2. Country of module assembly
3. Polysilicon source(s)
4. AD/CVD cash deposit rate (if applicable) for their specific entity
5. Whether pricing is DDP (all tariffs included) or FOB/ex-works (buyer bears tariff)

A quote that says "prices subject to tariff changes" is FOB-equivalent risk —
the buyer absorbs any tariff increase between quote and delivery.

## String Inverters

### Tariff Exposure
String inverters from Chinese manufacturers (Huawei, Sungrow, Growatt, Goodwe)
are subject to Section 301 tariffs. Rates have varied — confirm current rate.

String inverters from European manufacturers (SMA, Fronius, ABB/FIMER) assembled
outside China generally have lower or no Section 301 exposure.

String inverters from US-assembled manufacturers (Enphase — microinverters,
SolarEdge — hybrid manufacturing) have different tariff profiles.

### Practical Guidance
For utility-scale and large C&I projects, inverter tariff exposure can be
material. Confirm tariff-inclusive pricing or obtain freight forwarder
confirmation of applicable HTS code and duty rate.

## Racking Systems

### Steel and Aluminum Tariffs
- Section 232 tariffs apply to imported steel and aluminum
- Most racking systems contain both
- Domestic racking manufacturers (GameChange, Nextracker, Array Technologies,
  Unirac) are generally not subject to 232 tariffs on the finished product
- Racking from overseas manufacturers may carry 232 exposure plus any
  applicable anti-dumping duties

### IRA Domestic Content Bonus
The Inflation Reduction Act provides a bonus tax credit for projects meeting
domestic content requirements for iron, steel, and manufactured products.
Racking and structural components are included in this calculation. If the
project is targeting the domestic content bonus, confirm that racking meets
the applicable requirements — a lower-cost imported racking option may cost
more after the ITC reduction is considered.

## BESS (Battery Energy Storage)

Battery storage equipment, particularly lithium-ion cells from Chinese
manufacturers, carries significant tariff exposure including Section 301
tariffs. The tariff environment for batteries has been particularly volatile.

For any BESS procurement, obtain:
1. HTS classification confirmation from the vendor
2. Current duty rate confirmation
3. UFLPA compliance documentation for battery cells

## General Procurement Risk Management

For any project where equipment is being imported:

1. **Lock pricing DDP where possible** — requires vendor to absorb tariff risk
2. **Include tariff change language in purchase agreements** — specify who bears
   the cost of tariff changes between PO date and delivery date
3. **Build tariff buffer into project budgets** — a 5-10% buffer on imported
   equipment is reasonable given current policy volatility
4. **Confirm lead times account for potential customs holds** — UFLPA reviews
   can add 30-90 days to delivery timelines without warning
